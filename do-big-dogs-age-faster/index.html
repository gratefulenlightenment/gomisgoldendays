Understanding dog years

Do big dogs age faster?

By Gomi's human — written from 11+ years alongside Gomi (@gomithesuperyorkie), not a vet's office · Last updated July 20, 2026 · about a 5-minute read

The short answer: yes. Once puppyhood is over, larger dogs generally age faster than small dogs — and reach their golden years noticeably sooner.

Part of our complete guide to caring for a senior dog.

It's one of the quiet surprises of loving a dog. A tiny Yorkshire Terrier and a Great Dane can be born the same week, yet by the time they're seven years old, they're at completely different stages of life. The little one may still bounce around like a puppy, while the giant is easing into a gentle, senior pace. Understanding why helps you notice the changes that matter — and make the most of every golden day.

The first two years are the great equalizer

No matter the breed, all dogs pack an astonishing amount of growing into their first two years. A dog's first year is roughly equivalent to 15 human years, and by the end of the second year most dogs are close to a 24-year-old human. During this window, size barely changes the math — a Chihuahua and a Labrador are aging at a similar breakneck pace.

It's after year two that the paths split. From there, each additional calendar year adds a different number of "human years" depending on the dog's adult size — fewer for small dogs, many more for giant ones.

Why does size speed up aging?

Scientists are still untangling the full picture, but a few factors stand out:

  • Rapid early growth. Large dogs cram a huge amount of physical growth into a short time. That accelerated growth appears to come with a cost, wearing systems out sooner.
  • Growth hormones like IGF-1. Bigger dogs tend to have higher levels of insulin-like growth factor 1, a hormone linked in research to faster cellular aging and a higher rate of age-related disease.
  • Earlier onset of age-related conditions. Because they reach "senior" biology sooner, large and giant breeds often encounter joint, heart, and other age-related issues at a younger calendar age.

In other words, big dogs don't just live shorter lives on average — they move through each life stage on a faster clock.

Researchers have put numbers to this. In a large 2013 study in The American Naturalist, biologist Cornelia Kraus and colleagues found that roughly every extra 2 kg (about 4.4 lb) of body mass costs a dog around a month of life expectancy — and that big dogs die younger mainly because their risk of death climbs faster with age, not because they start out frailer (study, plain-English summary). Size doesn't just move the finish line closer — it speeds up the whole race.

What this means for caring for your dog

The practical takeaway isn't sad — it's useful. If you share your life with a large or giant breed, it's worth treating them as a "senior" a little earlier than you might expect:

  • Consider more frequent vet check-ups from around age 6, rather than waiting until 9 or 10.
  • Watch for subtle shifts in energy, appetite, sleep, stiffness, and mood — the small changes that quietly signal a new life stage. Our senior dog signs checklist covers what to look for.
  • Adjust exercise, diet, and comfort (softer bedding, easier access to favorite spots) as the golden years arrive.

Small-breed families get a longer runway, but the principle is the same: knowing roughly where your dog is on their timeline helps you give them what they need, when they need it.

Frequently asked questions

At what age is a large dog considered senior?

Large and giant breeds are often considered senior around 6 to 7 years old. Many small breeds aren't considered senior until about 9 to 11 years.

Is the "multiply by seven" rule ever accurate?

Rarely. Multiplying a dog's age by seven ignores both the fast early years and the effect of size. A size-aware chart — like the one above — gives a much closer estimate.

Does this mean I have less time with a big dog?

On average, large breeds do have shorter lifespans than small ones. But averages aren't destiny. Good care, a healthy weight, and regular vet visits all help — and every day, at every size, is worth noticing.

Every dog ages on their own schedule. This article is general information, not veterinary advice — for anything about your dog's health, please talk to your vet.
