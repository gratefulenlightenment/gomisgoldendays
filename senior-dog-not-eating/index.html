Caring for a senior dog

Why is my senior dog not eating?

By Gomi's human — written from 11+ years alongside Gomi (@gomithesuperyorkie), not a vet's office · Last updated July 20, 2026 · about a 5-minute read

When an older dog turns away from the food bowl, it's natural to worry. A dip in appetite can be minor — but in a senior dog it can also be the first quiet sign of something that needs attention. Here are seven common causes, gentle things you can try, and the signs that mean it's time to call your vet.

Part of our complete guide to caring for a senior dog.

Important: A senior dog that won't eat at all for about 24 hours — or refuses food alongside vomiting, diarrhea, weakness, or clear pain — should see a vet promptly. This article is general information to help you understand the possibilities, not a diagnosis. When in doubt, call your vet.

7 common reasons older dogs lose their appetite

  • Dental or mouth pain. Sore gums, a cracked tooth, or infection make chewing hurt. This is one of the most common — and most treatable — causes.
  • Nausea or an upset stomach. Digestive issues, constipation, or feeling queasy can quietly kill an appetite.
  • Pain elsewhere in the body. Arthritis can make bending down to a floor-level bowl uncomfortable, so a dog may simply avoid the effort.
  • A duller sense of smell and taste. Food drives largely by aroma, and both senses fade with age — meals may just seem less tempting.
  • Medication side effects. Some medicines reduce appetite or cause mild nausea. If a new prescription lines up with the change, mention it to your vet.
  • An underlying illness. Kidney, liver, dental, and other conditions — more common in seniors — often show up first as a fading appetite.
  • Stress or routine changes. A new home, a lost companion, travel, or cognitive changes can all put a dog off food for a while.

Because the list ranges from "sore tooth" to "serious illness," the pattern matters more than a single skipped meal. It helps to know your dog's life stage, too — you can estimate it with our dog age calculator, and appetite is one item on our broader senior dog signs checklist.

Appetite is worth taking seriously in an older dog for a simple reason: seniors have less reserve to fall back on. A skipped meal or two matters more at twelve than at two, and because age-related conditions often announce themselves first as a quiet loss of interest in food, vets treat a new appetite change as a reason to look closer rather than wait it out (AVMA: senior pet care). That's not cause for panic — most causes are manageable — but it is cause for attention.

Gentle things you can try at home

If your dog is otherwise bright and acting normally, these calm, non-medical tweaks are worth a try for a day or two:

  • Warm the food slightly. A few seconds of warmth releases the aroma and can make a meal far more appealing.
  • Offer softer or wet food. Easier on a sore mouth than hard kibble.
  • Raise the bowl. A bowl at chest height spares a stiff neck and achy joints.
  • Keep mealtimes calm and consistent. A quiet spot and a steady routine reduce stress-related fussiness.
  • Try hand-feeding a little. For some dogs, a bit of gentle encouragement rekindles interest.
  • Don't leave food out all day. Offer at set times so you can actually track how much is eaten.
Avoid sudden diet changes or "people food" as a fix without checking with your vet — rich or unfamiliar food can upset a senior stomach and mask what's really going on. Track how much and how often your dog eats; a written note or the Golden Days journal makes patterns easy to share at a check-up.

When to call the vet

Reach out to your vet — don't wait it out — if you notice any of these:

  • No food at all for around 24 hours (sooner for small or very old dogs).
  • Appetite loss with vomiting, diarrhea, or a lot of drinking.
  • Weakness, lethargy, or hiding.
  • Signs of mouth pain: drooling, dropping food, bad breath, or pawing at the face.
  • Noticeable weight loss over days or weeks.
  • Any change that comes on suddenly.

In seniors, appetite loss is frequently the earliest sign of a problem that's easier to treat when caught early. A phone call is always worth it.

Frequently asked questions

How long can a senior dog safely go without eating?

Not long. A healthy adult might skip a meal or a day, but for a senior — especially a small dog — contact your vet at around 24 hours of no food, or sooner if other symptoms appear.

My dog is drinking but not eating — is that better?

Staying hydrated is good, but refusing food while drinking (or drinking much more than usual) can still point to an underlying issue. Note it and check with your vet.

Could it just be old age?

Older dogs do sometimes eat a little less as their energy needs drop. But "old age" should be a conclusion your vet reaches after ruling things out — not an assumption, especially when the change is sudden.
